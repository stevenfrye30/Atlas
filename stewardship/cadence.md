# Cadence

This document articulates the operational disciplines for how editorial work proceeds across years and decades. It complements the editorial constitution (which articulates how entities are made) and the apprenticeship structure (which articulates how stewards are formed). Cadence is the temporal dimension of the work — when entities are made, when pauses occur, how the rhythm of stewardship sustains careful work without becoming production.

The disciplines below are guidance, not rule. They exist to support stewardship judgment, not to replace it.

## Why cadence matters

Once entity-making is recognized as the substance of continuity work, a specific psychological pressure emerges: the steward feels that more entities means more contribution. This pressure is sincere and corrosive. Sustained productive rhythm produces drift in atmospheric register, in editorial restraint, in adjacency discernment, in translation discipline. The drift is small per-entity and accumulates across years.

The architecture's commitment to slow growth is not aesthetic preference. It is structural defense against the drift that productive rhythm produces.

## Pacing

1. **No production schedule.** The architecture does not commit to producing entities on any timeline. Entities appear when the editorial work is genuinely complete.

2. **Variable rhythm.** Productive periods (when several entities are simultaneously warranted) and pause periods (when the labor is in re-reading, revision, deepening) alternate. The variation is structural.

3. **External pressures do not set cadence.** Reader expectations, institutional reporting, peer comparisons inform stewardship considerations but do not determine when entities are made.

4. **Internal pressure toward consistency is suspect.** A stewardship rhythm that is constant — the same level of editorial output every month — suggests organization around production rather than around the labor. The work's actual demands vary; the stewardship's rhythm should reflect that.

## Pauses

5. **Pauses are work.** A period without new entities, undertaken deliberately, is editorial labor in another form. The labor includes re-reading the existing corpus, revision, primary-source engagement, dialogue, documentation, apprentice formation, constitutional review.

6. **Pauses are articulated.** The stewardship records what specific work a pause is undertaking. The articulation prevents pause from becoming default neglect.

7. **Pause length is not predetermined.** Pauses end when the labor that warranted them is complete. Some last weeks; some last years.

8. **Re-reading existing entities is continuity work.** A steward who has not produced an entity in months but has re-read the corpus carefully has been doing the work.

9. **Sitting with deferred entities is continuity work.** A steward considering whether a candidate entity warrants making, across months of engagement with the underlying material, is producing future-stewardship-capacity even when no entity emerges.

## Entity selection

10. **An entity is warranted when the editorial work is genuinely complete.** The test: can the steward articulate, in advance of writing, what specifically this entity will hold that the existing corpus does not? If the articulation is generic, the entity is not yet warranted.

11. **"Not yet" is the default response to candidate entities.** Movement from "not yet" to "yes" requires specific editorial work to have matured.

12. **Refuse representation-completion pressure.** Entities are not added because a region "should" cover something or because an absence is uncomfortable. The architecture's incompleteness is structural.

13. **Notice when entities feel productive.** Entities that come together quickly often draw on existing knowledge rather than emerging from sustained engagement. This is sometimes correct; it is often a warning sign.

14. **Some candidate entities should never be made.** Sustained engagement with a candidate entity sometimes produces the recognition that the entity should not be made. The recognition is editorial work; the non-entity is the outcome.

## Gestation

15. **Candidate entities can mature for years.** A steward considering an entity may sit with the question across multiple readings of the source material, multiple stewardship dialogues, multiple seasons of stewardship work. The gestation is labor; the entity, when it emerges, reflects what the gestation produced.

16. **Record gestation.** When a candidate entity is identified but deferred, a brief decision record articulates the deferral and what would warrant return. The record makes the gestation visible across stewardship generations.

## Revision restraint

17. **Revise rarely and specifically.** Existing entities should not be revised unless the editorial work warrants change. General polishing, register updates, modernization should be refused.

18. **Substantial revision requires substantial reasoning.** A revision that changes what an entity claims requires articulation of why the original claim is no longer adequate. The articulation belongs in `decisions/`.

19. **Period markers can be preserved.** Older entities that show their writing-period are sometimes better left as artifacts than revised toward contemporary register. The corpus's history is part of what it preserves.

## Expansion restraint

20. **New regions are opened rarely and with stewardship consensus.** Opening a new region commits stewardship attention across years; it should not happen without sustained deliberation.

21. **Existing regions are deepened before new regions are opened.** A region with thinly developed material warrants further attention before stewardship spreads to additional regions.

22. **Stewardship competence bounds expansion.** The architecture renders only what current stewardship can hold with care. Material exceeding current competence remains deferred.

## Early warning signs

Stewards monitor for these patterns. Each is small individually; cumulatively they signal production drift.

23. **Editorial prose develops repeated phrasings.** Phrases that worked once become reflexive across multiple recent entities.

24. **Notes sections begin following templates.** What had been case-specific becomes formulaic.

25. **Adjacency choices follow patterns from earlier entities.** Reflexive structure rather than specific deliberation.

26. **Citation formats regularize in ways that lose specificity.** Templates glosses over edition-specific nuances.

27. **The steward feels behind.** The perception of being behind is the production-drift emotion. Investigating what behind-relative-to-what reveals the assumption.

28. **The steward enjoys publishing more than revising.** Preference for visible output over the more substantive labor of revision tracks drift.

29. **The steward begins thinking in terms of "this month's entities."** Temporal framing imports production-rhythm vocabulary the architecture's commitments resist.

## Atmosphere preservation

30. **Periodic atmospheric review.** Stewards periodically read recent entities alongside year-0 entities and ask whether the atmospheric register has remained stable. Drift accumulates invisibly without this comparison.

31. **Drift is corrected through dialogue.** When a steward's work shows drift, another steward articulates the perception. Drift correction is dialogical; it should not depend on individual self-criticism alone.

32. **The architecture's voice should disappear into the work.** Entity prose articulates the entity's content, not the architecture's commitments. Self-referential commentary is suspect.

## Production-pressure refusal

33. **Productive periods warrant skepticism.** When entities have been produced in close succession, the stewardship pauses to examine whether the production reflects genuine warrant or accumulated momentum.

34. **External output pressure is named.** When stewardship feels pressure to produce, the pressure is articulated explicitly so it can be considered rather than absorbed unconsciously.

35. **Some output expectations should be refused.** Foundation grants requiring specific output metrics may be incompatible with the architecture's commitments; refusing them is preferable to producing to specifications.

## On mortality and cadence

The steward's stewardship time is finite. The corpus will not be complete when the steward's work ends. The recognition can produce haste (more entities, faster) or restraint (fewer entities, more carefully). The architecture's discipline orients toward the second: what continues is what was carefully made; what was hastily made is what later stewardship revises or quietly leaves to fall into disuse.

The steward's contribution is shaped by what they did carefully, not by how much they did. The labor is to make what they pass on as careful as possible within the time available.

## On what continuity work is

Continuity work is the careful making of entities, the careful holding of what has been made, the careful formation of stewards who will continue the work, the careful articulation of disciplines that support the work, and the careful refusal of pressures that would convert the work into production. Each of these is the work; entity-making is one outcome among several that the work produces.

The labor is what matters; entities are what the labor leaves behind.

## What this document is not

This document is not a productivity system. It does not prescribe rates of work, target outputs, or evaluation metrics. It articulates the disciplines that protect editorial care across years.

This document is not anti-production. There are productive periods in any stewardship; the disciplines do not forbid them. They articulate when productive periods become production drift, and how the rhythm of stewardship can sustain careful work without industrializing it.

This document is provisional. Its articulations may need revision as stewardship experience reveals where they are inadequate.
