# Apprenticeship

This document articulates how new stewards are formed within ATLAS. It complements the editorial constitution: the constitution articulates the disciplines; this document articulates how the disciplines are transmitted to people who do not yet inhabit them.

The recommendations are small-scale, realistic, and non-institutional. They emerged from a simulated apprenticeship that surfaced where the constitution transmits well, where it transmits poorly, and where transmission depends on shared work rather than on documents.

## Why apprenticeship matters

The architecture's deepest disciplines are interpretive. Whether commentary has crossed into colonization of interpretation, whether an adjacency rises to inclusion, whether an entity has reached its proper depth — none of these is fully derivable from the constitution. Each requires inhabited judgment that develops through doing the work alongside people who have already developed it.

A new steward who reads the constitution carefully understands the disciplines as named principles. They do not yet inhabit them. The inhabitation occurs through apprenticeship: months and years of shared editorial work, dialogical review, and slow formation of shared sensibility.

Without apprenticeship, two failure modes occur:

- **Drift.** New stewards interpret disciplines through their own training. The architecture gradually becomes whatever the new stewardship brings rather than what the disciplines preserve.
- **Rigidity.** New stewards treat the constitution as a rule book. Editorial work that legitimately requires judgment freezes into mechanical compliance. The architecture loses adaptive capacity.

Apprenticeship is the structural defense against both.

## Onboarding (first weeks)

1. **Read everything before writing anything.** The new apprentice reads all existing entities, the constitution (`editorial-constitution.md`), the structural disciplines (`disciplines.md`), the editorial conventions, the relationship vocabulary, the workflow, and all decision records. Estimated time: two weeks of careful reading. The reading is not cursory; the apprentice should be able to articulate what each region contains and why.

2. **Read primary sources alongside the architecture's renderings.** The apprentice reads the materials the architecture engages — at minimum, the passages it renders, in primary form (with translation if needed). This grounds the architecture's editorial choices in encounter with the materials those choices respond to.

3. **First editorial work is review, not addition.** The apprentice reviews three to five existing entities and writes brief notes for the existing steward — what each entity is doing, what they would have done differently, what they don't understand. The exercise reveals where their sensibility differs from the existing stewardship and gives the existing steward early visibility into pressure points.

## Apprenticeship structures (first six months)

4. **Co-edit the first three new entities.** The apprentice drafts; the existing steward reviews; they discuss; they revise together. The discussions surface tacit considerations that solo work would not encounter.

5. **All editorial decisions for the first six months pass through dialogue.** Not just entity additions — adjacency proposals, revision decisions, translation choices, ontology questions. The apprentice's reasoning is articulated and engaged.

6. **Schedule structured discussions, not just review-as-needed.** Monthly conversations that take a specific case and work through it together. Structure ensures engagement does not depend on incidental encounter.

## Review practices (ongoing)

7. **Review at the level of disciplines, not at the level of correctness.** The reviewing steward asks: which disciplines does this draft engage? Are they engaged correctly? Where might the apprentice have drifted? The review is not "is this right" but "is the editorial work sustainable under the architecture's commitments."

8. **Articulate the implicit.** When the reviewing steward catches drift, they articulate the discipline that caught it — including the parts of the discipline that are not in the constitution. Tacit knowledge becomes progressively more articulate over time.

9. **Refuse with reasoning.** When the reviewing steward refuses an apprentice's proposal, they articulate why. The articulation is the formation; refusal without reasoning produces resentment without learning.

## Revision discussion

10. **Treat revisions as opportunities for shared work.** When an existing entity is revised, both stewards review the new draft. The review surfaces what the original draft was doing, what the revision changes, and whether the change preserves or modifies the architecture's editorial position.

11. **Document substantial revisions in `decisions/` with reasoning.** The record builds the architecture's interpretive history; future stewards see what was reconsidered and on what grounds.

## Using the constitution

12. **The constitution is shared vocabulary, not adjudication.** When apprentice and steward disagree, the constitution provides terms (omission discipline, adjacency sparseness, architectural invisibility) that make the disagreement legible. The constitution does not resolve; it makes resolution possible.

13. **Refer to specific cases in the corpus, not just to constitutional sections.** "Like *practice-li-mourning*'s Notes section" is more useful than "per Section IV.4." The cases ground the disciplines in inhabited practice.

14. **When the constitution feels inadequate, articulate why.** If a case reveals that the constitution does not cover the consideration that should govern the decision, that is information for future constitutional revision. Record it.

## Editorial collaboration

15. **Maintain stewardship plurality from the start.** Two stewards minimum; three to five preferred. Single-steward arrangements produce blind spots no one is positioned to catch.

16. **Avoid scaling stewardship until the practice has stabilized.** A stewardship of two to three that has worked together for two years is more capable than a stewardship of seven assembled from disparate backgrounds. Slow growth of stewardship matches slow growth of corpus.

17. **Lead stewardship rotates with apprenticeship maturity.** As the apprentice's sensibility matures, they take on more decision authority. Eventually they can review another new apprentice's work; at that point the apprenticeship has become stewardship in full sense.

## Protecting against drift and rigidity

18. **Drift correction is dialogical, not procedural.** When a steward notices drift in their own work or a colleague's, they articulate it and the dialogue addresses it. There is no adjudication mechanism beyond dialogue between formed stewards.

19. **Rigidity correction is also dialogical.** When a discipline is being applied too strictly — refusing legitimate editorial work to avoid violation — that is surfaced through dialogue. The constitution is corrigible; the disciplines are revisable through articulation of cases that reveal their inadequacy.

20. **Periodic constitutional review.** Approximately annually, the stewardship reviews the constitution against the year's editorial work. Where the constitution served, it stays. Where it produced fear or inflexibility, it is revised. Where it missed cases, it is extended.

## What apprenticeship cannot accomplish

Several things this structure does not produce:

- **Guarantee that the apprentice will mature into formed stewardship.** Some apprentices will not. The apprenticeship structure makes the maturation possible; it does not make it certain.
- **Prevent disagreements from persisting.** Stewards inhabiting the same disciplines can reach different judgments on specific cases. Persistent disagreement is sometimes resolved through time, sometimes through revision of the disciplines, sometimes by accepting that two formed sensibilities differ on the case.
- **Eliminate drift and rigidity entirely.** Both pressures are continuous. The structure manages them; it does not eliminate them.
- **Replace the substantial labor that stewardship requires.** Following these recommendations is itself substantial work — months of co-editorial labor, monthly structured dialogue, ongoing review. The labor is the substance; the architecture is what survives the labor.

## What this document is

This is one of the documents the architecture's continuity depends on. Future stewardships inheriting the work will read it; their interpretation of it will shape how new apprentices are formed; the cumulative effect across stewardship generations will substantially determine whether the architecture survives.

The document is therefore important and finite. It articulates apprenticeship as currently understood; it cannot anticipate what future stewards will discover about apprenticeship through their own work. Like the constitution, it is revisable — and probably should be revised, periodically, as the practice teaches what the document does not yet name.
